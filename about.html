Federal Privacy Law
HIPAA Privacy Notice
What HIPAA Is

The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996 (HIPAA) is a federal law that establishes national standards for the protection of certain health information. HIPAA restricts how health information may be used and shared and gives individuals specific rights regarding their health data.

How It Applies Here

Any health-related information you provide through this website or during our advisory engagement is handled in strict compliance with applicable HIPAA privacy standards. This includes any medical history, conditions, medications, or other health information submitted as part of an intake questionnaire or consultation.

Your health information will not be disclosed to any insurance carrier or third party without your written authorization prior to any formal application submission. You will be informed of what information is being shared, with whom, and for what purpose before any such disclosure occurs.

Your HIPAA Rights
  • The right to receive a notice of privacy practices
  • The right to access and review health information held about you
  • The right to request corrections to inaccurate information
  • The right to request restrictions on certain uses or disclosures
  • The right to receive an accounting of disclosures made on your behalf
  • The right to file a complaint if you believe your privacy rights have been violated
If you believe your health information privacy rights have been violated, you may file a complaint with the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services Office for Civil Rights. Filing a complaint will not affect your ability to obtain coverage or services through this practice.

Federal Consumer Protection Law
Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA)
What the FCRA Is

The Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) is a federal law that regulates how consumer reporting agencies collect, use, and share information about individuals. It gives consumers specific rights when their information is used to make decisions about them, including decisions related to insurance.

How It Applies to Your Insurance Application

In connection with a formal application for life insurance, the issuing insurance company may obtain a consumer report about you. This report can include information about your creditworthiness, credit standing, character, general reputation, personal characteristics, or mode of living.

If the insurance company takes an adverse action based in whole or in part on information in a consumer report, such as declining your application, offering a rated premium, or limiting your benefit, the company is required by federal law to notify you. That notice must include the name, address, and phone number of the consumer reporting agency that supplied the report.

Your Rights Under the FCRA
  • The right to be told if information in your consumer report was used against you
  • The right to request a copy of your consumer report from the reporting agency
  • The right to dispute inaccurate or incomplete information in the report
  • The right to add a statement of explanation to your file if a dispute is not resolved
  • The right to seek damages from violators of the FCRA
No consumer report will be requested on your behalf without your knowledge. You will be informed prior to any application submission. This advisory practice does not request or receive consumer reports directly.
Industry Disclosure
Medical Information Bureau (MIB)
What the MIB Is

The Medical Information Bureau (MIB) is a member-owned organization that operates as an information exchange among participating life and health insurance companies. Most individually underwritten life and health insurance carriers are MIB members.

How It Affects Your Application

When you apply for individually underwritten life or health insurance, the issuing carrier may request information from the MIB about prior insurance applications or claims you may have submitted with other member companies. This coded information is used during the underwriting process to identify inconsistencies and help prevent misrepresentation on applications, which helps keep premiums fair for all consumers.

The MIB may also create a record of your current application that future member companies may access if you apply elsewhere.

Your MIB Rights
  • You have the right to request a copy of any information the MIB has on file about you
  • You may contact the MIB directly at mib.com or by calling 1-866-692-6901
  • You have the right to dispute any inaccurate information in your MIB file
  • MIB information alone is not used to make a final underwriting decision
A formal MIB inquiry will not be initiated without your knowledge and will only occur in connection with a formal application submitted at your direction.
Underwriting Disclosure
Pharmacy Records (Rx History)
What a Pharmacy Records Check Is

Depending on the insurance carrier and the product applied for, the issuing company may request a report that includes your current and historical prescription drug records from participating pharmacies. This type of report is a standard underwriting tool used by many life insurance carriers.

How It Is Used

Prescription drug history is reviewed during underwriting to help the carrier correlate medications on file with health conditions disclosed on the application. It helps the carrier evaluate risk and ensure the accuracy of information provided. The report typically covers several years of prescription history from participating pharmacy networks.

Your Rights
  • A pharmacy records check will only be requested as part of a formal application submitted at your direction
  • You will be informed if such a check is required before your application is submitted
  • If an adverse underwriting decision is made based on this information, you will be notified and given the right to dispute inaccuracies
Pharmacy record checks may or may not be required depending on the specific carrier and product selected. Not every carrier uses this type of report for every application.
Underwriting Disclosure
Motor Vehicle Report (MVR)
What a Motor Vehicle Report Is

A Motor Vehicle Report (MVR) is a report obtained from your state Department of Motor Vehicles that provides a history of your driving record. It may include traffic violations, license suspensions or revocations, DUI or DWI convictions, accidents on record, and the current status of your driver's license.

How It Is Used in Life Insurance Underwriting

Some life insurance carriers review driving history as part of the underwriting process because certain driving patterns, such as multiple DUI convictions or a history of reckless driving, are considered indicators of risk. An MVR may be requested depending on the carrier's underwriting guidelines for the specific product applied for.

Your Rights
  • An MVR will only be requested as part of a formal application submitted at your direction
  • You will be informed if an MVR is required before your application is submitted
  • If an adverse underwriting action is taken based on MVR information, you have the right to be notified under applicable federal and state law
  • You may obtain a copy of your own driving record directly from your state DMV at any time
An MVR may or may not be required depending on the specific carrier and product. Not all carriers order motor vehicle reports for every applicant or product type.
